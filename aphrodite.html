Aphrodite


Aphrodite, an ancient Greek goddess celebrated for her association with love and beauty, was likened to Venus by the Romans. According to the Greek myth, her name derives from "aphros," meaning "foam." Hesiod's Theogony describes her birth from the sea's white foam generated when Uranus's severed genitals, discarded by his son Cronus, entered the sea. While Aphrodite was revered as a goddess of the sea and seafaring and was also honored in a martial context, particularly in places like Sparta, Thebes, and Cyprus, her primary domains were love, fertility, and occasionally, marriage. Despite her affiliation with prostitution, her public worship generally took on a solemn and austere nature.

Some scholars suggest that the worship of Aphrodite may have been influenced by Eastern traditions, as many of her attributes share similarities with ancient Middle Eastern goddesses like Ishtar and Astarte. Although she was referred to as "Cyprian" due to her association with the island of Cyprus, she had already undergone Hellenization by the time of Homer's writings. According to Homer, she was the daughter of Zeus and his consort Dione, located at Dodona. In the Odyssey, she was paired with Hephaestus but was romantically involved with Ares, the god of war, resulting in the birth of Harmonia, Phobos, Deimos, and Eros.

Among her mortal lovers, the most notable were Anchises, a Trojan shepherd with whom she had Aeneas, and Adonis, a Semitic nature deity and consort of Ishtar-Astarte. Adonis met a tragic end, killed by a boar during a hunt, and his death was lamented by women during the Adonia festival. Her connection to the realm of the dead is also evident in her association with Delphi.

Aphrodite's primary centers of worship were located in Paphos and Amathus on Cyprus, as well as on the island of Cythera, a Minoan colony where her cult likely originated in prehistoric times. In mainland Greece, Corinth stood as the principal hub of her worship. Her close relationship with Eros, the Graces (Charites), and the Horae (Seasons) emphasized her role in promoting fertility.
